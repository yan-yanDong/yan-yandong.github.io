About me

I’m now a Postdoctorial Fellow in the School of Science and Engineering (SSE) at The Chinese University of Hong Kong, Shenzhen (CUHK, Shenzhen).
Prior to this, I was a Research Fellow in the Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering (ECE) at the National University of Singapore (NUS), where I was hosted by Prof. Vincent Y. F. Tan.
In 2022, I earned my Ph.D. from School of Science and Engineering (SSE) at The Chinese University of Hong Kong, Shenzhen (CUHK,Shenzhen) supervised by Prof. Shenghao Yang.
In July 2017, I graduated with a Bachelor’s degree in Mathematics and Applied Mathematics from Jilin University.

Research

Research interests

My research interests lie in the following four aspects:

  • Multi-armed bandits

  • Finite-length block codes

  • Network coding

In general, I am interested in information theory, online learning, and network coding. During my doctoral studies, I focused on designing optimal finite-blocklength codes for binary symmetric channels under maximum likelihood decoding, aiming to understand the fundamental limits of reliable communication in the short blocklength regime. I also investigated network utility optimization in multi-flow wireless networks with network coding, analyzing the scalability of throughput and latency as the length of line networks increases, and comparing the performance of different coding schemes. As a postdoctoral researcher, I have expanded my interests to include multi-armed bandit problems, exploring both theoretical aspects and practical extensions under constraints such as switching costs and delayed feedback.

  • In finite-length coding, I have worked on finding optimal block codes that maximize the ML decoding probability for BSC through comparisons of ML decoding performance between codes.

  • In multi-armed bandits (MAB), I studied the theoretical analysis of the MAB problem with additional complexities, such as extra switching costs or delayed feedback beyond the traditional setting. Specifically, I developed theoretical performance lower bounds and designed efficient algorithms with low regret for both bandit and semi-bandit feedback scenarios, while accounting for switching costs incurred when changing decisions between rounds. Additionally, I am working on addressing the best arm identification (BAI) problem in the presence of feedback delays.

  • In network coding, I have worked on two major problems. First, I studied network utility optimization in multi-flow wireless networks when using BATS (BATched Sparse) codes—a variation of random linear network coding. We jointly optimized the BATS code parameters and network scheduling for all flows. Second, I conducted scalability analysis of throughput and latency as the line network length increases, comparing different coding schemes in a network affected by outages due to fading and interference. We compared the scalability of three schemes—random linear network coding, retransmission, and store-and-forward—providing valuable insights for the design of multihop wireless communication networks.
