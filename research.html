Yanyan Dong

Research Statement

I am broadly interested in information theory and online learning. During my doctoral studies, I focused on the open problem of verifying optimal finite-blocklength block codes under maximum likelihood (ML) decoding.
In particular, I investigated optimal codes with four codewords over a wide range of blocklengths for binary symmetric channels (BSCs).
I also analyzed the scalability of communication performance in line networks as the number of hops grows, under both latency and buffer constraints. This analysis was conducted in two settings: line networks composed of general discrete memoryless channels (DMCs) and those affected by slow fading with outage events.
In addition, I studied network utility optimization in multi-flow wireless networks employing network coding.
As a postdoctoral researcher, I have expanded my research to include multi-armed bandit (MAB) problems, with a focus on both theoretical foundations and practical extensions involving constraints such as switching costs and delayed feedback.

Online Learning

  • Adversarial Combinatorial Multi-Armed Bandit with Switching Cost

    I studied the adversarial combinatorial multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem where switching between selected arms across rounds incurs a cost. To characterize the inherent difficulty of this problem, I derived minimax lower bounds on the regret to understand the fundamental performance limits, and developed algorithms with provable upper bounds under both bandit and semi-bandit feedback. While the tightness of the bounds remains open, the results shed light on the complexity introduced by switching constraints and suggest directions for further refinement.

  • Multi-Armed Bandit with Delayed Feedback

    I am currently working on the best arm identification problem under delayed feedback, aiming to develop algorithms that can effectively accommodate such delays. The goal is to minimize the sample complexity in the fixed-confidence setting while accounting for feedback latency.

Optimal Finite-Blocklength Codes

  • Optimal Finite Length Codes for Binary Symmetric Channels (BSCs)

    As part of my interest in finite-length information theory, I have investigated the structure and performance of block codes with short lengths. In particular, I focused on identifying codes with four codewords that maximize the probability of correct decoding under maximum likelihood (ML) decoding for the binary symmetric channel (BSC). To enable rigorous comparisons, I developed a methodology for evaluating the ML decoding performance of codes with subtle structural differences. More broadly, my research aims to understand the performance limits of finite-length codes from diverse perspectives and to design efficient codes and decoding schemes tailored to the short blocklength regime.

Network Coding

  • Capacity of Line Networks with DMCs or slow-fading channels

    I conducted scalability analysis of throughput and latency as the line network length increases, comparing different coding schemes in a network affected by outages due to fading and interference. We compared the scalability of three schemes—random linear network coding, retransmission, and store-and-forward—providing valuable insights for the design of multihop wireless communication networks. Also, I have also took part in the study for the capacity scalability of line networks with DMCs under latency and buffer constraints.

  • Network Utility Maximization with Network Coding

    I studied network utility optimization in multi-flow wireless networks when using BATS (BATched Sparse) codes—a variation of random linear network coding. We jointly optimized the BATS code parameters and network scheduling for all flows.
